# M03 — Your business context

> **Module 3 of 20** · Part 1: Foundation
> **Estimated time:** 90 minutes (most of it: actually writing your file)
> **Outcome:** A finished `business_context.md` you'll attach to every AI run from this day forward.

## Premise

This is the single most important asset in the entire course. The document is the model's only window into who you are. A weak business context creates weak everything downstream.

> Spend an afternoon on this. Most people spend ten minutes and then complain that the AI sounds generic.

## Concept — The 12-question framework

Each answer should be 1–3 sentences. Specific, concrete, no marketing speak. The model reads what you write *literally*.

| # | Question | Why it matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| 1 | One-sentence company description | Forces a clear value statement. If you can't say it in a sentence, the model can't either. |
| 2 | Industry / niche | Disambiguates jargon. ("Retention" in SaaS ≠ retention in fitness.) |
| 3 | Time in business | Shapes tone. A 12-year operator and a 12-week founder write differently. |
| 4 | Customer transformation — *before* | The pain language goes straight into your hooks. |
| 5 | Customer transformation — *during* | Reveals the actual mechanism, not the marketing. |
| 6 | Customer transformation — *after* | The outcome that drives every CTA. |
| 7 | Customer transformation — *proof* | Concrete numbers / quotes the model can cite. |
| 8 | Core offer | Name, price band, deliverable, time-to-value. |
| 9 | Business model | Subscription? One-time? Performance fee? Models reason about cash. |
| 10 | Why you (not a competitor) | The differentiator the model must hit, not invent. |
| 11 | Voice and forbidden phrases | 5 words you say a lot, 5 you would never say. |
| 12 | Strategic narrative for this quarter | The single story all content should ladder up to. |

## Walkthrough — A concrete answer set

Here's a fictitious-but-plausible context for a B2B operator. Notice every answer is small, specific, and unambiguous:

```text
1. We help solo HVAC contractors in Canada land 5–10 net-new commercial accounts
   per quarter without cold calling.
2. Vertical lead-gen for trades. Not general B2B marketing.
3. 4 years.
4. Before: contractor relies on word-of-mouth + Google Ads. Pipeline is whatever showed up.
5. During: we run their LinkedIn outbound + a property-manager intent list, weekly cadence.
6. After: 90 days in, 5–10 booked discovery calls per month from named target accounts.
7. Proof: 14 contractors, $2.4M in net-new commercial revenue tracked through year 1.
8. "HVAC Pipeline Engine" — done-for-you outbound, $3,500/mo + setup.
9. Subscription, 6-month minimum. Cash up front for setup.
10. We refuse to take more than 3 contractors per metro. Geographic exclusivity.
11. Say a lot: pipeline, named-account, dispatch, route density.
    Never say: synergy, leverage, journey, ecosystem.
12. This quarter: "You don't need more leads, you need fewer better ones."
```

Read that out loud. It would be hard for *anyone* — human or AI — to write generically with that on the page.

## Exercise — Write yours

1. Open `01_Business_Context/business_context.md` in your editor.
2. Block 90 minutes. Disable Slack, email, your phone.
3. Answer all 12 questions. If an answer runs over 3 sentences, you are explaining — cut.
4. Save. Attach this file to every AI run from now on. **No exceptions.**

**Acceptance test:** read your finished file out loud. Could a competitor write the same answers about their business? If yes, the answers are too generic. Add specifics until "no."

> ⚠️ **Pitfall.** If you find yourself writing aspirational answers ("we *will* be the leader in..."), stop. The model writes in present tense. Aspirations create slop. Write only what is true today.

## Knowledge check

1. Why do most people spend 10 minutes on this and then complain about generic output?
2. What's the test for whether an answer is too generic?
3. Why do questions 4–7 split customer transformation into four sub-questions instead of one?

### Answer key

1. Because a thin business context produces thin output. The model can only echo what it sees. Ten generic minutes give it ten generic minutes of fuel.
2. "Could a competitor write the same answers about their business?" If yes, the answer is generic. Add specificity until the answer would be wrong if a competitor copied it.
3. Each sub-question maps to a different content beat: hooks (before), mechanisms (during), outcomes (after), proof (during/after). Splitting them gives the model handles for different output types.

## Takeaway

Your AI is an obedient employee with selective amnesia. The business_context.md file is the onboarding document you give it on its first day, every day, forever.
