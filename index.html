The problem

Your AI output sounds generic. Your model isn't broken — it's starving.

Most people prompt AI with one line and paste the result. The output is vaguely correct, vaguely on-brand, and entirely forgettable. The internet has a name for this: AI slop.

Slop is not a model problem. It's an input problem. The model has nothing to anchor on except its training data — the average of the internet — so it produces the average of the internet back.

AI sounds like the internet because it is trained on the internet. Train it on your own work, and it sounds like your own work.— the core principle

The fix is upstream. The fix is the system around the prompt.

The proof, in 30 seconds

Same prompt. Same model. Three radically different results.

We asked the same model to "write a tweet about pricing mistakes founders make" three times. The only thing that changed was what we attached.

Run A

Prompt only

"Many founders underprice. Don't leave money on the table. Charge what you're worth! #startup #pricing"
Run B

+ Business context

"Charging hourly turned my agency into a busy job. The day we switched to monthly retainers, profit per hour 2.5x. Same work. Different invoice."
Run C

+ 200 past tweets

"Hourly rates put a ceiling on you. Retainers put a floor under you. Same hours. Different ladder."

The model didn't change. The system around it did. Run C uses the 4-folder system you'll learn in the free guide.

The system

Three inputs decide every AI output. Most people skip two.

1 — The who

Business context

One file. The single source of truth about who you are, who buys from you, and what you sell. Attach it to every prompt.

2 — The past

Data

Your historical work, organized by output type. Voice, rhythm, and taste live here. The model copies them.

3 — The ask

Prompt

Role, process, constraints, format. Length is the lever most people get wrong: longer prompts → tighter outputs.

Skip any one and you get slop. Provide all three and the same model that wrote the slop now writes in your voice.

FAQ

Common questions

Do I need to know how to code?

No. The vibe-coding chapters assume zero programming background. They give you the mental model (web apps in five layers, CRUD as a thinking tool) and then walk you through prompting an AI to write the code while you review and ship.

Which AI do I need? Claude? ChatGPT? Gemini?

Any modern frontier model works for the content side. For coding we recommend Claude Code or Antigravity (Google's IDE that bundles Gemini). The framework is portable — switch tools without rewriting your prompts.

How long until I see results?

The 15-minute experiment in the free guide proves the principle the same day. Most students publish their first on-voice tweets in week 1 and ship the portfolio project in week 2. Lead-gen SaaS-shape projects typically take 3–4 weekends.

What if I already have an audience and content?

You're in the best position. You have data — the most expensive part of the system. The course shows you how to feed it to the model so the model writes in your established voice instead of overriding it.

Is this just prompt engineering?

No. Most courses teach prompts. This one teaches the system around the prompts so output quality compounds over time: business context, organized data, reusable prompt templates, and (in Pro) starter code repos. The prompt is the smallest part.
