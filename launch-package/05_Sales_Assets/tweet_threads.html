Thread 1 — The slop diagnosis

Most "AI is bad at writing" complaints are not about the model.

They're about the absence of three things.

A short thread on what's actually missing 👇

—

1/ Watch what happens when you ask Claude (or GPT, or Gemini) to "write a tweet about X."

You get a 280-character platitude.

Hashtags. Hedging. The voice of LinkedIn at 2pm.

The model isn't broken. You're starving it.

—

2/ Three inputs decide every AI output:

• Business context (the who)
• Data (the past)
• Prompt (the ask)

Skip any one and you get slop.

Provide all three and the same model that wrote the slop now writes in your voice.

—

3/ Most people only ever provide #3.

So the model has to guess at the other two — and the average of "everyone in your industry" is the most common guess.

That's why your output sounds like everyone else's output.

—

4/ Try this experiment.

Same model, three runs:

A. Just a prompt.
B. + 5 sentences about your business.
C. + 5 sentences + 20 of your best past posts.

Read the three side by side.

You'll never look at AI the same way again.

Thread 2 — The prompt-length law

Most people make their AI prompts longer when the output gets worse.

That's exactly backwards.

—

1/ Long prompts produce SHORTER, sharper output.
Short prompts produce LONGER, vaguer output.

Sounds wrong. Then you watch it happen.

—

2/ A long, structured prompt constrains the model.

Role. Phases. Constraints. Format.

Nowhere to wander.

—

3/ A short prompt is a hand-wave.

The model has to guess at all of those.

It guesses by producing more text — covering its bases.

—

4/ Here's the structure of a real production prompt:

PHASE 1 — silently build a voice profile
PHASE 2 — generate 4 drafts using the profile
PHASE 3 — return in this exact format
End: a one-paragraph self-audit

Every word does work.

—

5/ Try the structure on your own writing today.

Drop your business_context.md and 50 past posts into Claude.

Use a 1-page prompt instead of a 1-line one.

Compare.

Thread 3 — The compound interest is in the latency

The thing nobody tells you about content with AI:

It doesn't 20x your output.

It 20x's your willingness to publish ideas you weren't sure about.

—

1/ Most of your best ideas are already in your head.

You've argued them on calls. Said them in Slack a hundred times.

What's stopping you isn't the idea. It's the time-to-publish.

—

2/ With the right system, the time from "idea I'm sure of" → "published in your voice" goes from ~4 hours to ~11 minutes.

Cut latency by 20x and you don't 20x your output.

You change which ideas you're willing to publish.

—

3/ Some of those will be wrong.

Some will land you a $4,200/month account from a 2-line tweet (real student, real number).

You don't know in advance which is which.

—

4/ The compound interest is in the latency.

Not the volume. Not the prompt. Not the model.

The time between "I have an idea" and "it's live."

Tips for posting these

  • Don't post all three the same week. Space them 3–5 days apart.
  • Edit the names and numbers. "Aman" and "$4,200" are placeholders. Use real students if you have them; if you don't, remove the social proof section entirely. Fabricated case studies kill trust faster than no case studies.
  • Pin the strongest one to your profile during launch week.
  • Quote-tweet replies with mini-explanations when people push back. Public objections handled gracefully sell better than the original thread.
